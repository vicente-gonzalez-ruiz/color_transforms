1 Definition

The \(\text {RGB}\)-DCT is a DCT applied to a vector of \(3\) elements (in our case, a \(\text {RGB}\) pixel) defined by \begin {equation} \begin {bmatrix} \text {DCT}_0 \\ \text {DCT}_1 \\ \text {DCT}_2 \end {bmatrix} = \begin {bmatrix} 0.57735027 & 0.70710678 & 0.40824829 \\ 0.57735027 & 0.0 & -0.81649658 \\ 0.57735027 & -0.70710678 & 0.40824829 \end {bmatrix} \begin {bmatrix} \text {R} \\ \text {G} \\ \text {B} \end {bmatrix}, \end {equation} and the inverse transform by \begin {equation} \begin {bmatrix} \text {R} \\ \text {G} \\ \text {B} \end {bmatrix} = \begin {bmatrix} 0.57735027 & 0.57735027 & 0.57735027 \\ 0.70710678 & 0.0 & -0.70710678 \\ 0.40824829 & -0.81649658 & 0.40824829 \end {bmatrix} \begin {bmatrix} \text {DCT}_0 \\ \text {DCT}_1 \\ \text {DCT}_2 \end {bmatrix}. \end {equation} See the notebook 3-Channels DCT to see how to compute the filter’s coefficients.

When applied to the \(\text {RGB}\) color domain, we will move from this domain to the color-DCT domain that have also 3 components, that we will denote by \(\text {DCT}_0\), \(\text {DCT}_1\) and \(\text {DCT}_2\). If the decorrelation is effective, most of the energy will be concentrated in \(\text {DCT}_0\), which represents the average energy of the image (luminance).

Notice also that the DCT is orthonormal, and therefore, the matrix of the forward transform is the transpose of the matrix of the backward transform [1]. This also means that the contribution of the synthesis filters (which define the inverse transform) to the reconstructed signal are independent and have exactly the unity gain.1

2 Quantization in the 3-DCT domain

The synthesis filters of DCT are orthogonal (their contributions to the reconstructed signal are independent) and have exactly the unity gain.2 Therefore, without considering that the entropy coding stage can performs better for some subbands, the optimal quantization pattern should be \begin {equation} \Delta _{\text {DCT0}} = \Delta _{\text {DCT1}} = \Delta _{\text {DCT2}}. \end {equation} See the notebook Color-DCT image compression.

Notice that to find the gains (of any 1D transform) we can compute the energy of the signal generated by the inverse transform of the impulse discrete 1D signal \begin {equation} \delta _{i}(x) = \left \{ \begin {array}{ll} 1 & \text {if $i=x$}\\ 0 & \text {otherwise}, \end {array} \right . \end {equation} where the energy of a discrete signal \(x\) is defined as \begin {equation} \langle x, x\rangle = \sum _{i}{x_i^2}. \end {equation}

If we consider that the RD curve can be affeced by the compresibility of the subbands, a better solution to find the optimal RD curve would consist in selecting the quantization step size for each subband that produce the same slope in the corresponding RD channel curve.
